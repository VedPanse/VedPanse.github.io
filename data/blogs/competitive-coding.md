# Competitive Coding Has Been Weirdly Important For Me

![Algorithms, puzzles, and late-night coding sessions](assets/banners/competitive.png)

### How It Started

I first got into coding puzzles in 2020 through Advent of Code. At the time I wasn’t thinking about “competitive programming” at all. I just saw people online solving these daily programming puzzles every December and thought it looked fun. So I decided to try a few problems myself. I was using Python back then because it was the language I was most comfortable with and it made it easy to quickly try ideas without worrying too much about syntax or structure.

What I didn’t expect was how addictive the problems would become. Some days the solution would come together quickly and I would feel clever for a few minutes. Other days I would spend way too long staring at the same problem wondering why my brain refused to see the trick. But that moment when the idea finally clicks and the code works is weirdly satisfying. That alone was enough to keep me coming back to more problems.

### What These Puzzles Actually Train

Over time I realized that these puzzles train a very specific kind of thinking. You read a problem, you misunderstand it the first time, you try a naive solution, it fails, and then you slowly refine the idea until it starts making sense. That process happens over and over again.

Competitive coding forces you to read problems carefully, think about constraints, and break things down into algorithms and data structures instead of vague ideas. There are no frameworks, no UI, and no large architecture decisions involved. It’s just a problem and your ability to translate it into logic. That simplicity is what makes it so good for training your thinking.

After doing enough of these puzzles, you start to notice patterns faster. You get less intimidated by weird problem statements. You also get used to being stuck for a while, which turns out to be a surprisingly useful skill when working on real projects or research problems.

### Moving From Python To Kotlin

When I started, I solved everything in Python. It was fast to write and easy to debug, which makes it perfect for puzzles. But over time I began solving problems in Kotlin instead. I was already using Kotlin in other projects, and I realized that it works surprisingly well for algorithmic problems too.

Kotlin is expressive enough that you can write solutions quickly, but still structured enough that things don’t turn into a mess. After a while it just became my default language when I sat down to solve problems. Switching languages also made me realize that the real challenge isn’t the syntax. Once you understand the algorithm, translating it into code is the easy part.

### The 2AM JetBrains Contest

Recently I participated in the Blackbox Cup hosted by JetBrains. The contest started at 2 AM in my timezone and ran until 7 AM, which is honestly a terrible time to start solving algorithm problems. But competitions like that have a strange energy to them. You know that people all over the world are solving the exact same problems at the same time.

So I stayed up, made some coffee, opened my editor, and just went for it. For the next five hours it was just me, the problems, and a lot of thinking. Sometimes the ideas came quickly. Sometimes I had to fight with a problem for way longer than I expected. By the end of it I was completely exhausted, but also strangely energized from the experience.

There’s something very satisfying about spending hours wrestling with difficult problems and eventually getting the solution to work.

### Why I Still Do It

I don’t really do competitive coding for rankings or leaderboards. For me it’s more like mental training. It keeps my problem-solving muscles sharp and forces me to think carefully about algorithms, efficiency, and edge cases.

What I’ve noticed is that the thinking style from these puzzles carries over into other areas. When I’m building systems, debugging strange behavior, or trying to understand a complicated research idea, the process feels very similar. You stare at the problem, try a few ideas, fail a couple of times, and eventually something clicks.

Competitive coding just gives you a lot of practice with that process. And the more practice you get, the less intimidating difficult problems start to feel.
