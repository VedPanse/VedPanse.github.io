# Why I’m Becoming Increasingly Interested in Optical Computing

![Light might become the next fundamental medium for computation](assets/banners/optical.png)

### A Growing Curiosity

Over the past few months, I’ve found myself becoming increasingly fascinated by optical computing. It started as casual curiosity while reading about alternatives to traditional silicon-based processors, but the more I learned, the more it began to feel like one of those technological directions that could fundamentally change how we build computers.

For decades, most computing progress has been driven by improvements in electronic transistors. Moore’s Law allowed us to pack more and more transistors onto chips, enabling everything from personal computers to modern AI systems. But as transistor scaling slows down and energy costs rise, it’s becoming clear that we may eventually need entirely new ways to compute.

Optical computing is one of the ideas that keeps appearing in that conversation.


### Computation with Light

Instead of using electrons moving through circuits, optical systems use light—photons—to perform computation. Light travels extremely fast, produces very little heat, and allows multiple signals to pass through the same medium simultaneously. In theory, this makes it possible for optical systems to perform certain kinds of computation far more efficiently than traditional electronic hardware.

What makes this particularly interesting to me is its potential impact on artificial intelligence.

Modern AI models rely heavily on matrix multiplication and large-scale linear algebra. These operations become incredibly expensive when executed electronically at scale. Optical systems, however, can naturally perform some of these transformations through the physical interaction of light, which opens the possibility of dramatically reducing the energy required for AI workloads.

### The Memory Bottleneck

At the same time, I’m aware that computation itself isn’t the only challenge. In modern computing systems, memory and data movement are often the real bottlenecks. Moving data between memory and compute units consumes enormous amounts of energy and time, sometimes more than the computation itself.

This is one reason I’ve also become increasingly intrigued by compute-in-memory (CIM) approaches. Instead of separating memory and computation, CIM architectures attempt to perform certain operations directly where the data already resides.

While optical computing and CIM address different parts of the problem, I find it fascinating to imagine how architectural ideas like these might evolve together over time.

### Quiet Inventions That Change Everything

Not every technological shift arrives as a dramatic revolution. Sometimes the changes that reshape computing happen quietly, through architectural improvements that slowly become unavoidable.

Compute-in-memory feels like it could be one of those silent inventions. It’s the kind of idea that might not capture immediate mainstream attention, but over time could fundamentally alter how systems are designed.

If optical computing fulfills even a fraction of its promise, it could reshape the hardware layer of AI. Combined with innovations in memory architecture, it might push us toward entirely new kinds of computing systems.

### Why This Excites Me

Of course, the field is still early. There are many practical challenges—precision, programmability, manufacturing complexity, and integration with existing electronic systems. But that uncertainty is also what makes it exciting.

It reminds me of earlier moments in computing history, when technologies like GPUs or neural networks were still considered niche ideas before they eventually became foundational.

For me, optical computing sits at the intersection of several things I care deeply about: artificial intelligence, hardware innovation, and the search for fundamentally better ways to build intelligent systems.

Most of the computing world still revolves around electrons. But it’s hard not to wonder what might happen if the future of computation ends up being written in light.
